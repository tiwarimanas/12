Play Introduction

"On the Face of It" is a powerful one-act play that explores the profound themes of human connection, disability, and perception.

Main Themes

Disability & Perception

How society views and treats people with physical differences

Loneliness & Isolation

The universal human experience of feeling alone and misunderstood

Friendship & Understanding

The transformative power of genuine human connection

Inner vs. Outer Beauty

The importance of character over physical appearance

By Manas Tiwari

Characters

Derry (Derek)

Age: 14 years old

Physical: Half of his face burned by acid

Initial Personality: Withdrawn, defiant, bitter, self-conscious

Character Arc: Transforms into a more confident, assertive person seeking genuine connection

Key Traits: Sensitive, intelligent, perceptive, capable of growth

Mr. Lamb

Age: Elderly

Physical: Has a tin leg (lost real leg in war)

Personality: Wise, accepting, optimistic, lonely but hopeful

Role: Mentor figure who helps Derry see beyond superficial appearances

Key Traits: Patient, understanding, philosophical, resilient despite disability

By Manas Tiwari

Scene 1: The Encounter

Setting: Mr. Lamb's garden

Summary

Fourteen-year-old Derry enters Mr. Lamb's garden by climbing over the wall, thinking it's empty. He's startled when Mr. Lamb speaks to him about fallen crab apples. Derry wants to leave immediately, but Mr. Lamb encourages him to stay.

Derry reveals his deep insecurity about his acid-burned face, believing everyone fears and pities him. Mr. Lamb shares that he has a tin leg from the war and is called 'Lamey-Lamb' by children, but he doesn't mind.

They discuss the difference between weeds and flowers, with Mr. Lamb suggesting it's all just perspective. The scene ends with Derry promising to return to help pick crab apples.

By Manas Tiwari

Scene 1: Analysis

Character Development

Derry begins defensive and hostile but gradually becomes more open. Mr. Lamb establishes himself as wise and accepting.

Scene 2: At Home

Setting: Derry's house

Summary

Derry returns home and tells his mother about Mr. Lamb. His mother forbids him from going back, having heard rumors about the old man. She believes Mr. Lamb is dangerous and that Derry is 'best off' staying home.

Derry argues passionately that Mr. Lamb talks about things that matter, things nobody else has ever said. He declares that his feelings have nothing to do with his face anymore - it's about what he thinks, feels, and wants to discover.

Despite his mother's prohibition, Derry asserts his independence and runs back to the garden, stating that if he doesn't go back now, he'll never go anywhere in the world again.

By Manas Tiwari

Scene 2: Analysis

Key Themes Explored

  • Family protection vs. independence
  • Breaking free from limitations
  • Self-assertion and growth

Important Quotes

"It's got nothing to do with my face and what I look like. I don't care about that and it isn't important."
"It's what I think and feel and what I want to see and find out and hear."
"If I don't go back there, I'll never go anywhere in this world again."
"And I want the world....I want it....I want it...."

Character Development

Derry shows remarkable growth, asserting his independence and focusing on inner qualities rather than appearance. This scene marks his transformation from a withdrawn, bitter boy to someone who actively seeks meaningful experiences and connections.

By Manas Tiwari

Scene 3: The Tragic Return

Setting: Mr. Lamb's garden

Summary

Mr. Lamb is alone in his garden, picking crab apples from the tree using a ladder. He falls from the ladder and lies motionless on the ground.

Just then, Derry arrives, excited to show that he kept his promise and returned despite opposition. He finds Mr. Lamb lying on the ground, apparently dead.

Derry kneels beside him, calling out 'Lamey-Lamb' affectionately and telling him that he did come back as promised. The play ends with Derry weeping over his newfound friend's body.

By Manas Tiwari

Scene 3: Analysis

Key Themes Explored

  • Tragic irony
  • The fragility of human connection
  • Death and loss
  • The impact of brief but meaningful relationships

Important Quotes

"You see, you see! I came back. You said I wouldn't and they said....but I came back."
"Mr Lamb, It's all right....You fell....I'm here, Mr Lamb, It's all right."
"I came back. Lamey-Lamb. I did.....come back."

Irony and Symbolism

The tragic irony is profound - just as Derry has found the courage to pursue meaningful relationships and assert his independence, his newfound friend dies. The fall from the ladder symbolizes the fragility of life and human connections.

Derry's transformation is complete - he has overcome his fears and limitations, though tragically his mentor is gone. His calling Mr. Lamb "Lamey-Lamb" shows his complete acceptance and affection.

By Manas Tiwari

Themes & Messages

Disability and Perception

The play explores how society treats people with physical disabilities and how this affects their self-perception and place in the world.

Loneliness and Isolation

Both characters suffer from profound loneliness, but they find connection and understanding through shared experiences and acceptance.

Inner Beauty vs. Outer Appearance

The story emphasizes that true worth and beauty come from character, thoughts, and feelings, not physical appearance.

Transformation and Growth

Derry's character undergoes significant positive change through his brief but meaningful interaction with Mr. Lamb.

The Power of Human Connection

Even brief, meaningful relationships can have profound impacts on our lives and personal growth.

Courage and Independence

The importance of asserting one's independence and having the courage to seek new experiences despite fear or opposition.

By Manas Tiwari

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

  • Physical disabilities do not define a person's worth or potential
  • True beauty lies in character, kindness, and understanding
  • Brief encounters can have life-changing impacts
  • Overcoming prejudice requires courage and open-mindedness
  • Human connection is essential for personal growth and healing

Relevance to Modern Society

"On the Face of It" remains deeply relevant today as society continues to grapple with issues of inclusion, acceptance, and the treatment of people with disabilities. The play's message about looking beyond superficial appearances to find the humanity in everyone is timeless and universal.

Susan Hill's masterful portrayal of two lonely souls finding each other reminds us of the transformative power of empathy, understanding, and genuine human connection.
